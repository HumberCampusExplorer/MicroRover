Welcome! Our students are to reliably demonstrate the ability to:

  1. diagnose, solve, troubleshoot, and document technical problems involving computing devices using appropriate methodologies.
  2. integrate multiple software and hardware components using appropriate network architecture.
  3. participate in analyzing, planning, designing, and developing the architecture of computing devices and systems.
  4. plan, install, configure, modify, test, and maintain a variety of computer systems to meet functional requirements.
  5. apply principles of digital and analog circuits to the implementation of embedded computing devices.
  6. analyze, build, test, implement, and maintain applications.
  7. evaluate and document security issues associated with a variety of computing devices and propose alternatives to increase product reliability.
  8. articulate, defend, and conform to workplace expectations found in technology environments.
  9. contribute to the successful completion of the project applying the project management principles in use.

The CENG capstone project consists of:

  1. CENG 317 Hardware Production Technology (building a microprocessor based sensor/effector node),
  2. CENG 319 Software Project (building a mobile application); and,
  3. CENG 355 Computer Systems Project (integration of the work in the previous two courses):

A common introduction to microcontrollers is through the Lego NXT ROBOTC Curriculum. We will aim to make headway in a simplified project by building an independent microcontroller based sensor/effector node that could be integrated in the following way: This will give us some idea of what runs connected devices all over including vehicle systems and infotainment, PLCs, our phones, Lego Mindstorms, Arduino, Raspberry Pi as well as learn about the prototyping tools available at Humber. Based on the ATTiny85 It will drive in a straight line until it encounters an object following which it will reverse and turn before continuing straight again. The workshop milestones are:

  1. Kit and workstation familiarization.
  2. Blinking Light Emitting Diode (LED).
  3. Soldering Printed Circuit Board (PCB) together, and verifying it with test equipment.
  4. Assembly of rover complete with blinking LED powered by the lithium ion cell.
  5. Coding/uploading the appropriate program to run the rover.

Blinking LED

  1. Use your USB A to USB MicroB cable to upload blink to your trinket to confirm that it works:
    1. Log into a computer in J232. (If you are using your own computer, download the installer from arduino.cc)
    2. Install the Adafruit Windows Drivers.
    3. Follow these instructions including the integrated development environment setup until your development board blinks.
    4. Although you may be able to right away choose File->Examples->01.Basics->Blink, having the drivers and libraries installed will help get that code onto your device.
    5. My recommendation is to always verify (the checkmark button) your code prior to uploading (the right arrow button) since you only have a 5 second window to upload (after either plugging in the USB cable or pressing the reset button) and if you have yet to verify, that step is included in the upload.

Soldering PCB

  1. Please put on your glasses for safety as we will be soldering and using side cutters (despite this please make sure not to send to parts flying from your workspace). To solder these parts:
    1. Take a length of solid core hookup wire (from your student supplied parts kit) and strip a longer section of insulation than usual.
    2. Use pliers to create a 90 degree bend.
    3. Insert the short end of the bend into one of the two spots (vias) shown that are used to transfer the signal from one side of the board to the other.
    4. You may make two (as shown) or use the same one twice by soldering cutting and then reinserting in the other location:
    Solder these onto the provided PCB (laid out and made by Kelly and Vlad - Eagle files and Bill of Materials) from both sides then solder the resistors onto the provided PCB from both sides: Trim the via and resistor leads, then solder the 3 pin and 5 pin sockets: Next solder the headers (this photo is of a slightly different PCB but the idea is the same): Next solder the terminal posts/turrets. Next solder the 2 pin sockets to the opposite side of the PCB as photographed by Brandeen: The IR LED (clear) will eventually be trimmed to go in the socket connected to the 100 Ω resistor while the IR sensor (dark coloured) will eventually be trimmed to go into the socket connected to the 100 kΩ resistor.
  2. Solder the provided headers onto the trinket.
  3. Solder the provided headers onto the trinket backpack.

    5. Connect the jumper across both pins to enable the battery backpack. The jumper has a couple of different purposes:
      1. The most useful purpose is to use it to disconnect power from the mirco servos such that your are able to have a finished micro rover on your desk and program it without it driving away. This is also a handy way of having it when you are not using it such as when it is charging but for storage you should remove the lithium cell.
      2. The second purpose is that when it discoonects the power from the lithium ion cell it also alows you to directly power the rover from the VBAT turret/terminal.

Coding/Uploading appropriate code

    Kelly found that the example code in the software servo library works reasonably for the Micro Rover, thus:
  1. Once again load the Arduino IDE and choose Sketch->Include Library->Manage Libraries...
  3. In the "Filter your search ..." field type softservo
  4. Click on "More info" once.
  5. Click on "Install".
  6. Alternatively you may follow the library installation instructions discussed here or download and install it as a .zip.
  7. Once it is installed, choose File->Examples->Adafruit SoftServo->Trinket Knob
  8. Choose verify and then press the reset button on your trinket and then quickly click upload such that it completes in your 5 second window.
  9. To understand and tweak the code, discuss with your colleagues.
  10. The repository has the code running on the prototype.
